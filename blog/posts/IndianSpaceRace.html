The Indian Space-Race

India In the Space race and ISRO's impact on the Space industry

For most of the modern era, the Space Race has been between two main competitors, the Soviet Union (Russia) and the United States. Both superpowers rushed to prove their technological superiority over the other during the Cold War, ultimately culminating in NASA’s Apollo Missions to the moon. But with the rise of corporations such as Elon Musk’s SpaceX and Jeff Bezos's Blue Origin, aerospace technology is no longer reserved for the major world powers. However, other corporations and governments are starting to take major steps in the Space industry. Just last year, China was able to send a satellite to the unexplored territories of the moon. Now, a new potential superpower in the Space Race has emerged. ISRO, India’s Aerospace program

(The Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle, Credit; Adobe Stock)

The Cost of Exploration

During the Cold War era and even today, NASA and other companies have been dumping fortunes to create some of their rockets, often with some failures as well. In 2023, it was reported that NASA had spent $24 Billion U.S. dollars on their programs, with limited success. By contrast, ISRO had a mere $1.5 Billion.

What makes this even more shocking is that NASA’s budget had increased by $1.3 Billion, more than India has ever spent on any project. With just 73 million US dollars, India was able to send an orbiter to Mars through the MOM project (Mars Orbiter Mission). When India launched its Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV) It cost less than the Hollywood film Gravity, at only $31 Million. India’s advancements in more affordable rocket technology are paving the way to safer and better space travel and exploration.

(Chandrayaan 3, The first rover to reach the Moon’s South Pole, Credit; TIMES NOW)

The Grand Achievements

Founded during the Cold War, like many national SPACE programs, ISRO has started to skyrocket only relatively recently. In 2008, it made the breakthrough that would change what we know about the Moon, when Chandrayaan 1, India’s first successful moon landing, discovered the presence of water on the moon's surface. This breakthrough discovery changed the way we think about the moon, and with this discovery, we know that the moon is habitable.

Another spacecraft, known as Mangalyaan 1, was launched at a meager cost (see paragraph 2) and made India the only country to ever enter the Martian Atmosphere on its first attempt, in stark contrast, the United States Mars observer cost $813 million and was a failure, forcing the US the create the Mars Global Surveyor, though as a lower cost. The most important mission happened just recently when last year, Chandrayaan 3, which became the first-ever rocket to ever land on the south pole of the Moon. India's great achievements as a government space program are quite amazing considering that the budget has just started gaining pace, while NASA’s budget peaked 50 years ago.

Conclusion

As the advancements in technology in both corporate and government space agencies continue, there is no telling what humanity will accomplish in the next decade. As promised by People on Mars and colonies on the moon, there is no telling what will happen. China even plans to land people on the moon by the end of the decade, and Space X’s Rideshares are scheduled to happen by this August. But one thing is certain, India and ISRO will be a significant part of the next age of exploration, as humanity challenges the final frontier, Space.
